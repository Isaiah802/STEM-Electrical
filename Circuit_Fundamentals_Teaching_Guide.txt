SECTION 1: THE BIG THREE (Voltage, Current, Resistance)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Think of electricity like water flowing through pipes...

┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  VOLTAGE (V) = Electrical Pressure                                   │
│                                                                       │
│  • Measured in: Volts (V)                                           │
│  • Water analogy: Water pressure in the pipe                        │
│  • Symbol: V or E                                                   │
│  • Example: Our battery is 14.8V (pushes electrons with 14.8V      │
│             of "pressure")                                          │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  CURRENT (I) = Flow Rate                                             │
│                                                                       │
│  • Measured in: Amperes or Amps (A), milliamps (mA)                │
│  • Water analogy: How much water flows per second                   │
│  • Symbol: I                                                        │
│  • Example: Our flight controller uses 250mA = 0.25A                │
│             (250 milliamps of electrons flowing through it)         │
│  • Conversion: 1000 mA = 1 A                                        │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  RESISTANCE (R) = Opposition to Flow                                 │
│                                                                       │
│  • Measured in: Ohms (Ω)                                            │
│  • Water analogy: Narrow pipe restricts water flow                  │
│  • Symbol: R                                                        │
│  • Example: A resistor that limits current flow                     │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

SECTION 2: OHM'S LAW (The Most Important Formula)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

OHM'S LAW states the relationship between Voltage, Current, and Resistance:

    ╔═══════════════════╗
    ║   V = I × R       ║  ← THE MAGIC FORMULA
    ╚═══════════════════╝

Where:
  V = Voltage (Volts)
  I = Current (Amps)
  R = Resistance (Ohms)

Rearranged forms:
  I = V / R    (Find current if you know voltage and resistance)
  R = V / I    (Find resistance if you know voltage and current)


MEMORY TRICK - "Ohm's Law Triangle":

         V
        ╱ ╲
       ╱   ╲
      ╱─────╲
     I   ×   R

Cover what you want to find, and you see the formula!
  • Cover V: You see I × R  →  V = I × R
  • Cover I: You see V / R  →  I = V / R
  • Cover R: You see V / I  →  R = V / I


EXAMPLE FROM OUR UAV:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Question: If our servo draws 180mA when moving, and it's powered by 5V,
          what is its effective resistance?

Given:
  V = 5V
  I = 180mA = 0.18A  (convert to amps!)

Find R:
  R = V / I
  R = 5V / 0.18A
  R = 27.78 Ohms

Answer: The servo has an effective resistance of ~28Ω when moving.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Find: R = ?

SECTION 3: ELECTRICAL POWER (How Much Energy?)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

POWER measures how fast electrical energy is used or converted.

    ╔═══════════════════╗
    ║   P = V × I       ║  ← POWER FORMULA
    ╚═══════════════════╝

Where:
  P = Power (Watts)
  V = Voltage (Volts)
  I = Current (Amps)

Other forms (using Ohm's Law):
  P = I² × R
  P = V² / R


WHY WE CARE:
  • Power tells us how much energy a component uses
  • Higher power = more battery drain
  • Higher power = more heat generation
  • We need to ensure our BECs can supply enough power


EXAMPLE FROM OUR UAV - Motor Power:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Question: At 50% throttle, our motor draws 10A from the 14.8V battery.
          How much power is it using?

Given:
  V = 14.8V
  I = 10A

Find P:
  P = V × I
  P = 14.8V × 10A
  P = 148 Watts

Answer: The motor uses 148W at 50% throttle.


EXAMPLE FROM OUR UAV - BEC Capacity:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Question: Our ESC has a 5V 3A BEC. What is the maximum power it can supply?

Given:
  V = 5V
  I = 3A (max)

Find P:
  P = V × I
  P = 5V × 3A
  P = 15 Watts

Answer: The BEC can supply up to 15W of power.

This is WHY we calculated our system needs!
  • Flight controller: 5V × 0.25A = 1.25W
  • GPS: 5V × 0.06A = 0.3W
  • Receiver: 5V × 0.09A = 0.45W
  • Servos (all moving): 5V × 0.72A = 3.6W
  • Arduino + Sensors: 5V × 0.263A = 1.32W
  TOTAL = 6.92W

But 15W > 6.92W, so we're safe, right?
WRONG! Peak servo current can exceed 3A → BEC overload → WHY WE NEED DUAL-BEC!

SECTION 4: BATTERY CAPACITY (How Long Will It Last?)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Battery capacity tells us how much charge a battery can store.

Measured in: Amp-Hours (Ah) or milliamp-hours (mAh)
  • 1 Ah = 1000 mAh
  • Our battery: 2200 mAh = 2.2 Ah

Meaning: The battery can supply 2200mA for 1 hour
         OR 1100mA for 2 hours
         OR 4400mA for 0.5 hours (30 minutes)

    ╔════════════════════════════╗
    ║  Runtime (hours) = Capacity (Ah) / Current Draw (A)  ║
    ╚════════════════════════════╝


EXAMPLE FROM OUR UAV - Flight Time Calculation:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Question: Our motor draws 10A at cruise. Our battery is 2200mAh. How long
          can we fly?

Given:
  Battery Capacity = 2200 mAh = 2.2 Ah
  Current Draw = 10A

Find Runtime:
  Runtime = Capacity / Current
  Runtime = 2.2 Ah / 10A
  Runtime = 0.22 hours = 13.2 minutes

BUT WAIT! We should NEVER fully drain a LiPo battery!
  Safe limit: Use only 80% of capacity
  Usable capacity: 2200mAh × 0.8 = 1760 mAh = 1.76 Ah
  
  Safe Runtime = 1.76 Ah / 10A = 0.176 hours = 10.6 minutes

Answer: Safe flight time is about 10-11 minutes at cruise power.


CONVERTING UNITS:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Our 5V system draws 1383mA typical. How long can it run on the battery?

Step 1: Convert mA to A
  1383 mA ÷ 1000 = 1.383 A

Step 2: BUT the battery is 14.8V, and the 5V system is after conversion!
  We need to account for efficiency losses (~85% efficient)
  
  Power at 5V: P = 5V × 1.383A = 6.92W
  Current from 14.8V battery: I = 6.92W / 14.8V = 0.468A
  
Step 3: Calculate runtime
  Runtime = 1.76 Ah / 0.468A = 3.76 hours
  
This is why the 5V electronics are NOT the limiting factor - the motor is!

SECTION 5: SERIES vs PARALLEL CIRCUITS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  SERIES: Components in a single line (one path for current)          │
│                                                                       │
│      ─────[R1]─────[R2]─────[R3]─────                               │
│                                                                       │
│  • Current is THE SAME through all components                        │
│  • Voltage DIVIDES across components                                 │
│  • Total Resistance: R_total = R1 + R2 + R3                         │
│                                                                       │
│  Example: Battery cells in series (4S LiPo)                         │
│    Each cell: 3.7V                                                   │
│    4 in series: 3.7V + 3.7V + 3.7V + 3.7V = 14.8V                  │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  PARALLEL: Components on separate branches (multiple paths)          │
│                                                                       │
│           ┌────[R1]────┐                                            │
│      ─────┤            ├─────                                        │
│           │  [R2]      │                                            │
│           └────[R3]────┘                                            │
│                                                                       │
│  • Voltage is THE SAME across all components                         │
│  • Current DIVIDES among branches                                    │
│  • Total Resistance: 1/R_total = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3                │
│                                                                       │
│  Example: All our 5V components connected to BEC                    │
│    Each device gets 5V                                               │
│    Total current = FC + GPS + RX + Arduino + Servos                 │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘


OUR UAV POWER SYSTEM IS PARALLEL:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

        5V BEC ────┬───→ Flight Controller (250mA)
        (Rail 1)   ├───→ GPS Module (60mA)
                   ├───→ ELRS Receiver (90mA)
                   └───→ Arduino System (263mA)
                   
        Total Current = 250 + 60 + 90 + 263 = 663 mA

ALL devices get 5V (parallel voltage rule)
TOTAL current is the SUM (parallel current rule)

SECTION 6: WIRE GAUGE & CURRENT CAPACITY
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Wires have resistance! Thicker wire = less resistance = can carry more current.

Wire sizes are measured in AWG (American Wire Gauge):
  • SMALLER number = THICKER wire = MORE current capacity
  • LARGER number = THINNER wire = LESS current capacity

    ╔═══════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
    ║  AWG  │  Diameter  │  Max Current  │  Our Use    ║
    ╠═══════════════════════════════════════════════════╣
    ║  12   │  2.05mm    │  25-30A       │  (too thick)║
    ║  14   │  1.63mm    │  15-20A       │  Battery →  ║
    ║       │            │               │  ESC/UBEC   ║
    ║  16   │  1.29mm    │  10-13A       │  Motor wires║
    ║  18   │  1.02mm    │  7-10A        │  5V Power   ║
    ║       │            │               │  Distribution║
    ║  20   │  0.81mm    │  5A           │  Servo power║
    ║  22   │  0.64mm    │  3A           │  Signal wires║
    ║  24   │  0.51mm    │  2A           │  Sensor I2C ║
    ║  26   │  0.40mm    │  1A           │  UART/SPI   ║
    ╚═══════════════════════════════════════════════════╝


WHY THIS MATTERS:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If wire is too thin for the current:
  • Wire heats up (I² × R power dissipation)
  • Voltage drop occurs (V_drop = I × R_wire)
  • Can cause fire! 🔥

If wire is too thick:
  • Wastes money
  • Adds unnecessary weight to UAV
  • Harder to work with (stiff, bulky)


EXAMPLE FROM OUR UAV - Battery to ESC Wire:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Question: Our motor can draw up to 30A burst. What wire gauge do we need
          from battery to ESC?

Given:
  Max Current = 30A

From table: 14 AWG can handle 15-20A (not enough!)
            12 AWG can handle 25-30A (just barely enough)
            
Recommendation: Use 12 AWG or 14 AWG with short run
                (Our ESC comes with XT60 and appropriate wire pre-attached)

Answer: 12-14 AWG wire is appropriate.


EXAMPLE FROM OUR UAV - 5V Power Rail:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Question: Rail 1 carries max 3A (ESC BEC limit). What wire gauge?

Given:
  Max Current = 3A

From table: 22 AWG handles 3A (exactly at limit - risky!)
            20 AWG handles 5A (comfortable margin)
            18 AWG handles 7-10A (oversized but safe)

Recommendation: Use 18-20 AWG for 5V power distribution

Answer: 18 AWG provides best balance of capacity and flexibility.

SECTION 7: VOLTAGE REGULATION & BECs
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

BEC = Battery Elimination Circuit
  • Takes high voltage input (14.8V from battery)
  • Outputs stable low voltage (5V for electronics)
  • Allows us to use ONE battery for everything

┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  LINEAR REGULATOR (Not used in our system)                           │
│                                                                       │
│  14.8V ──→ [Linear Reg] ──→ 5V                                      │
│                                                                       │
│  • Simple, cheap                                                     │
│  • Wastes energy as heat: P_wasted = (V_in - V_out) × I            │
│  • Example: (14.8V - 5V) × 3A = 29.4W wasted as heat! 🔥            │
│  • Efficiency: ~40-60%                                               │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  SWITCHING REGULATOR / UBEC (What we use!)                          │
│                                                                       │
│  14.8V ──→ [Switching Reg] ──→ 5V                                   │
│                                                                       │
│  • More complex, more expensive                                      │
│  • Efficient energy conversion                                       │
│  • Efficiency: ~85-95%                                               │
│  • Minimal heat generation                                           │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘


POWER CONSERVATION:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In an ideal world: P_in = P_out (energy is conserved)

  P_in = V_in × I_in
  P_out = V_out × I_out
  
For switching regulator (assume 85% efficient):
  P_out = P_in × 0.85


EXAMPLE FROM OUR UAV - BEC Current Calculation:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Question: Our 5V system draws 3A. How much current does the BEC draw from
          the 14.8V battery? (Assume 85% efficiency)

Step 1: Calculate output power
  P_out = V_out × I_out
  P_out = 5V × 3A = 15W

Step 2: Calculate input power (accounting for losses)
  P_in = P_out / efficiency
  P_in = 15W / 0.85 = 17.65W

Step 3: Calculate input current
  I_in = P_in / V_in
  I_in = 17.65W / 14.8V = 1.19A

Answer: The BEC draws 1.19A from the 14.8V battery to supply 3A at 5V.

KEY INSIGHT: High voltage → Low current (that's why we use 14.8V battery!)

SECTION 8: REAL SYSTEM DESIGN - WHY DUAL-BEC?
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Now let's put it all together with OUR ACTUAL DESIGN DECISION.

SINGLE-BEC CONFIGURATION (Original plan - REJECTED):
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  14.8V Battery
     │
     ▼
  40A ESC with 5V 3A BEC
     │
     ├────→ Motor (14.8V)
     │
     └────→ 5V Rail (3A max)
            │
            ├──→ Flight Controller (250mA)
            ├──→ GPS (60mA)
            ├──→ Receiver (90mA)
            ├──→ Arduino + Sensors (263mA)
            └──→ 4× Servos (720mA moving, 1200mA peak!)
            
  PROBLEM CALCULATION:
    Typical: 250 + 60 + 90 + 263 + 720 = 1383 mA ✓ Under 3A limit
    Peak: 250 + 60 + 90 + 263 + 1200 = 1863 mA ✓ Still under 3A
    
    BUT... what if ONE servo stalls (jammed control surface)?
      One stalled servo: 650mA
      Three moving servos: 3 × 300mA = 900mA
      Other electronics: 250 + 60 + 90 + 263 = 663mA
      
      TOTAL = 650 + 900 + 663 = 2213 mA ✓ Still under 3A!
      
    BUT... what if TWO servos moving aggressively + normal load?
      Two servos peak: 2 × 300mA = 600mA
      Two servos idle: 2 × 10mA = 20mA
      Other electronics: 663mA
      
      TOTAL during aggressive turn = 1283 mA ✓ SAFE!
      
    SO WHY DID WE REJECT THIS?
      1. NO SAFETY MARGIN - operating at 60-75% of max continuously
      2. BEC THERMAL STRESS - constant high current = heat = premature failure
      3. VOLTAGE SAG - under heavy load, 5V drops to 4.8V → brownouts
      4. SINGLE POINT OF FAILURE - if BEC fails, you lose EVERYTHING


DUAL-BEC CONFIGURATION (Our design - APPROVED):
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  14.8V Battery
     │
     ▼
  XT60 Y-Harness Splitter
     │
     ├────→ 40A ESC with 5V 3A BEC
     │      │
     │      ├────→ Motor (14.8V)
     │      │
     │      └────→ Rail 1: Flight-Critical (3A available)
     │             │
     │             ├──→ Flight Controller (250mA)
     │             ├──→ GPS (60mA)
     │             ├──→ Receiver (90mA)
     │             └──→ Arduino + Sensors (263mA)
     │                  TOTAL: 663 mA (22% of 3A capacity) ✓ SAFE!
     │
     └────→ External 5V 6A UBEC
            │
            └────→ Rail 2: Servo Power (6A available)
                   │
                   └──→ 4× Servos (720mA typ, 1200mA peak)
                        PEAK: 1200 mA (20% of 6A capacity) ✓ SAFE!
                        
  ADVANTAGES:
    ✓ Flight-critical systems isolated from servo current spikes
    ✓ Each rail has 3× safety margin
    ✓ If servo BEC fails, flight controller still powered → controlled landing
    ✓ No voltage sag on critical systems
    ✓ Professional aerospace-grade redundancy


THE MATH THAT PROVED WE NEEDED DUAL-BEC:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  ESC BEC Capacity: 5V × 3A = 15W available
  
  Flight Critical Load: 5V × 0.663A = 3.3W (22% utilization) ✓
  Servo Load (peak): 5V × 1.2A = 6W (40% utilization at peak) ✓
  
  Single-BEC Peak: 3.3W + 6W = 9.3W (62% utilization) - Marginal!
  Dual-BEC Peak: Rail 1 = 3.3W (22%), Rail 2 = 6W (20%) - Excellent!
  
  Cost of External UBEC: $12-15
  Cost of Dual-BEC Architecture: $12-15 total (just the UBEC!)
  Cost of crashing due to BEC failure: $500+ (entire project)
  
  DECISION: $12 insurance policy → APPROVED!

  4. Have students solve problems with their actual calculators
  5. Show the consequences: "If we used 26 AWG for motor wire, it would
     literally catch fire. Let me show you the math..."

COMMON MISTAKES TO ADDRESS:

  • Mixing up series vs parallel rules
  • Not accounting for efficiency losses in BECs
  • Using 100% battery capacity instead of 80% safe limit
