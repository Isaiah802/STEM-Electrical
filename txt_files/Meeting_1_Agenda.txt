   SECTION 3: SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE LECTURE (0:15 - 0:45) - Teach
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

OBJECTIVE: Every team member understands power flow and design principles.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PART 3A: Draw the Block Diagram (15 minutes)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Start at the top of whiteboard. Draw as you explain. Use different colors.

"Let's start at the source of all power: our OVONIC 4S LiPo battery."

[Draw battery at top]

"4S means 4 cells in series. Each cell is 3.7V nominal, so:
  3.7V × 4 = 14.8V nominal voltage
  Fully charged: 4.2V per cell = 16.8V total
  Empty: 3.3V per cell = 13.2V total (never go below this - battery damage)
  Capacity: 2200mAh = 2.2Ah
  This battery can deliver over 286 amps burst (we won't, but it can)"

[Draw line down from battery, split into two paths]

"Power splits into two main paths:

Path 1: The ESC - Electronic Speed Controller"
[Draw ESC on left]
"This does two things:
  1. Provides high-power 14.8V to the motor (10-30 amps)
  2. Has a built-in BEC - Battery Eliminator Circuit - that gives us 5V @ 3A"

[Draw motor below ESC]

"Motor draws the most power - 10 to 18 amps typical, up to 30 amps full throttle.
That's 14.8V × 18A = 266 watts of power. This is why the motor dominates our power budget."

[Draw BEC output coming from ESC]

"The ESC's BEC gives us a 5V rail for electronics. 5V at 3A maximum.
This is Rail 1 - our Flight-Critical rail."

[Draw Rail 1 components: Flight Controller, GPS, Receiver, Arduino]

"Rail 1 powers:
  - MATEK F405-WMN Flight Controller: The brain. 250mA typical.
  - BN-220 GPS: Navigation. 60mA.
  - ELRS Receiver: Radio link to ground station. 90mA.
  - Arduino Nano: Research payload brain. 30mA.
  - Sensors: BME280 and MQ135. 160mA combined."

[Write currents next to each component, sum at bottom]

"Total: 663mA. We have 3000mA available from the BEC.
That leaves 2337mA margin. That's 78% headroom. Excellent, right?"

[Pause for effect]

"Wrong. Because I haven't told you about the servos yet."

[Draw 4 servos]

"We have 4 servos:
  - 2 ailerons (roll control)
  - 1 elevator (pitch control)
  - 1 rudder (yaw control)
  
A single servo draws:
  - 10mA idle (just sitting there)
  - 180mA moving (normal operation)
  - 300mA peak (fast movement)
  - 650mA stalled (control surface blocked or jammed)

In flight, all 4 servos move constantly. That's 4 × 180mA = 720mA typical, 1200mA peak."

[Write on whiteboard]

"663mA (electronics) + 720mA (servos) = 1383mA total typical draw.
We have 3000mA. Still okay, right? 1617mA margin."

[Write in red]

"But what if a servo stalls? Imagine a control surface gets jammed.
One servo tries to move, can't, draws 650mA.
Now: 663 + 650 + (3 × 180) = 1853mA. Still under 3A.

But what if TWO servos stall? Or THREE?
Now we're at 2500mA or more. We've exceeded the BEC capacity."

[Draw X through the circuit]

"What happens when you try to draw more current than a BEC can provide?
  1. Voltage sags - drops below 5V
  2. Flight controller browns out - resets
  3. You lose control
  4. You crash"

[Let that sink in for 3 seconds]

"This is called a single point of failure. Everything depends on one BEC."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PART 3B: Introduce the Solution (10 minutes)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[Draw second path from battery]

"The solution: Add a second BEC. We call this Dual-BEC Architecture."

[Draw External UBEC on right side of diagram]

"External UBEC:
  - Input: 14.8V from battery (via XT60 splitter)
  - Output: 5V @ 6A (twice the capacity of ESC BEC)
  - This becomes Rail 2: Servo Power Rail"

[Draw servos connected to Rail 2 for power]
[Draw servo signal wires going to Flight Controller]

"Now the servos get power from Rail 2, signal from Flight Controller.
This is called power/signal separation. It's standard aerospace practice."

[Write on whiteboard]

RAIL 1 (ESC BEC, 3A):          RAIL 2 (External BEC, 6A):
  - Flight Controller              - 4× Servos
  - GPS
  - Receiver
  - Arduino + Sensors
  
  Total: 663mA                     Total: 720-1200mA
  Margin: 2337mA (78%)             Margin: 4800mA (80%)

"Now, even if ALL FOUR servos stall simultaneously (1 × 650 + 3 × 300 = 1550mA),
we still have margin on Rail 2, and Rail 1 is completely unaffected.

Flight Controller voltage: STABLE.
GPS lock: MAINTAINED.
Control link: ACTIVE.

If Rail 2 fails completely, the flight controller stays powered. We can't move control
surfaces, but we can glide to a controlled landing.

If Rail 1 fails, servos keep working, but we lose the brain - catastrophic.
So we protect Rail 1 at all costs."

[Circle on whiteboard]

"This is ISOLATION. High-current loads cannot affect flight-critical systems."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PART 3C: Design Principles (5 minutes)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[Write on whiteboard, explain each]

"Five design principles guide our electrical architecture:

1. ISOLATION
   Flight-critical systems are isolated from high-current loads.
   Servo power rail is separate from FC power rail.

2. REDUNDANCY
   If servo BEC fails, FC stays alive.
   If FC fails... we're in trouble, so we protect FC at all costs.

3. COMMON GROUND
   All systems share the same ground reference.
   Battery negative is the master ground for everything.
   This prevents ground loops and signal noise.

4. NOISE ISOLATION
   Motor wires carry high-frequency noise from ESC switching.
   Keep motor wires AWAY from:
     - GPS antenna
     - Receiver antenna
     - Analog sensors (MQ135)
   Twist motor wires together to reduce electromagnetic interference.

5. POWER MARGIN
   We size everything with headroom.
   If a BEC can provide 3A, we only use 1.4A typical.
   This accounts for component tolerance, aging, and unexpected spikes.
   Real aerospace systems use 50-100% margin. We're doing the same."

[Turn to team]

"Questions on the architecture before we make our technical decision?"

[Take questions. This is important - make sure everyone understands before moving forward]

"We have a decision to make. I'm going to present three options, then we'll discuss and vote."

[Refer to Power Budget document, Section 5]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
OPTION A: Single BEC Configuration
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

"Use ESC BEC for everything. Simplest wiring.

Pros:
  - Simple
  - Minimal wiring
  - One less component

Cons:
  - Only 1137mA margin under peak load (38%)
  - Servo stall can exceed BEC capacity
  - Single point of failure
  - Risk of brownout → FC reset → crash
  - No engineering margin

My assessment: UNSAFE for portfolio-level aerospace work.
This is acceptable for a toy quadcopter. Not for a serious UAV."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
OPTION B: Dual-BEC Configuration (RECOMMENDED)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

"Add external 5V 6A UBEC for servos. Professional power distribution.

Pros:
  - Rail 1: 78% margin (flight-critical systems protected)
  - Rail 2: 80% margin (servos can handle simultaneous stall)
  - Isolation: Servo load cannot affect FC
  - Redundancy: Servo BEC failure doesn't kill FC
  - Professional aerospace practice
  - Strong portfolio demonstration

Cons:
  - Slightly more complex wiring
  - Need to purchase external BEC (~$10-15)
  - Additional component to integrate

Cost: $15 for UBEC
Benefit: Insurance against mid-flight power failure and loss of $500 aircraft

My assessment: This is the CORRECT engineering choice.
This is what I would do if this were my job at Lockheed Martin or Boeing."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
OPTION C: Hybrid Configuration
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

"Similar to Option B, but Arduino powered from Rail 2 instead of Rail 1.

This is acceptable if you want maximum isolation. Research payload failure cannot
affect flight systems. But adds complexity for marginal benefit."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FACILITATE DISCUSSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

"Thoughts? Concerns? Devil's advocate arguments?"

[Let team discuss for 5 minutes]

Common questions you might get:

Q: "Do we really need the extra margin?"
A: "In engineering, you don't size for typical case. You size for worst case. What if
a control surface freezes due to icing? What if a servo fails and draws excessive current?
Margin protects us when unexpected things happen. And unexpected things ALWAYS happen."

Q: "Can we just test with one BEC first and add second later if needed?"
A: "No. If we test with insufficient power architecture and encounter brownouts during
testing, we've just made our debugging 10× harder. Are bugs due to software? Wiring?
Configuration? Or just insufficient power? We eliminate power as a variable by doing it
right from the start."

Q: "How much will the external BEC cost?"
A: "$10-15 on Amazon. Hobbywing 5V 6A UBEC is $12. Given our total project budget is
$500, this is 2.4% additional cost for significant risk mitigation. Worth it."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VOTE & COMMIT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

"Let's vote. Show of hands:

Option A (Single BEC): [Should be 0-1 hands]
Option B (Dual BEC):   [Should be majority]
Option C (Hybrid):     [Maybe 0-2 hands]"

If vote is not unanimous for Option B, use this:

"I hear the concerns about added complexity. But fundamentally, this is a question of
risk tolerance. Option A has a >50% chance of causing brownout during aggressive
maneuvering based on the power budget calculations. That's unacceptable.

As Electrical Lead, my recommendation is Option B. This is my formal engineering
decision. Does anyone have a SAFETY or TECHNICAL concern that would override this?"

[Address any technical concerns. If none:]

"Then we're implementing Option B: Dual-BEC architecture. I'll order the external UBEC
today. It'll arrive in 2-3 days, and we'll integrate it in Week 1."

[Write on whiteboard in large letters:]

DECISION: DUAL-BEC ARCHITECTURE (OPTION B) ✓

"This is now our baseline electrical architecture. All wiring diagrams, testing procedures,
and documentation will reflect this design."

LIPO BATTERY SAFETY (10 minutes)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[Hold up LiPo battery IN safety bag]

"This is an OVONIC 4S 2200mAh LiPo battery. It stores 32.56 watt-hours of energy.
If mishandled, it can enter thermal runaway - an uncontrollable chemical reaction
that burns at over 1000°C. LiPo fires cannot be extinguished with water. They burn for
10-30 minutes and release toxic smoke.

Google 'LiPo fire' after this meeting. Watch a few videos. Take this seriously.

[Refer to Safety Protocol document, Section 1]

Key rules:

NEVER charge unattended. NEVER.
  If you're charging and need to leave, stop charging, disconnect, store in bag.

NEVER discharge below 3.3V per cell.
  Our battery management will cut power at 3.5V/cell (14.0V total).

NEVER overcharge above 4.2V per cell.
  The B6 charger handles this automatically, but still monitor.

NEVER use a swollen or damaged battery.
  If you see swelling or damaged wires, report immediately. Do not charge or use.

ALWAYS store in LiPo safety bag.
  This bag contains fire if battery ignites. It's not perfect, but it's critical.

ALWAYS charge on non-flammable surface.
  Metal, ceramic, concrete. NOT wood, carpet, or plastic.

[Demonstrate LiPo charger]

I'll now show you the proper charging procedure.

[Walk through B6 charger setup - see Safety Protocol]

1. Inspect battery (no swelling, no damage)
2. Place in safety bag on metal surface
3. Connect balance plug (5-pin JST-XH)
4. Connect main power (XT60)
5. Set charger: LiPo Balance Charge, 4S, 1.0A charge rate
6. Press START, verify 4S 14.8V, press START again
7. Monitor during entire charge (60-120 minutes)
8. When done, disconnect balance plug first, then main plug
9. Let cool 15 minutes before use

If battery becomes hot (>50°C), smells unusual, or swells during charging:
  STOP IMMEDIATELY. Disconnect. Move outdoors if safe. Monitor for 1 hour.

[Point to fire extinguisher]

Fire extinguisher is here. ABC rated, works on LiPo fires (barely). If LiPo catches fire,
evacuate, call 911, let it burn out outdoors if possible.

Questions on LiPo safety?"

[Take questions]

Questions on electrical safety?"

Appendix C: Leadership Tips (For You)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Being a good technical leader:

✓ BE CLEAR: Say exactly what needs to happen, by when, and who's responsible.
✓ BE CONFIDENT: You know more than your team. They're looking to you for guidance.
✓ BE SUPPORTIVE: When someone gets stuck, help them learn, don't just fix it.
✓ BE FIRM: If safety is compromised, shut it down immediately. No negotiations.
✓ BE ACCOUNTABLE: If the project fails, it's on you. Own it.

✗ DON'T micromanage: Give ownership, then get out of the way.
✗ DON'T be a bottleneck: Empower team to make decisions within their subsystems.
✗ DON'T ignore problems: Address issues immediately, don't let them fester.
✗ DON'T take all the work: Your job is to LEAD, not do everything yourself.

Remember:
  - This is a portfolio project for everyone, including you
  - Your leadership will be more impressive than perfect technical work
  - Employers want to see: systems thinking, communication, organization
  - This project demonstrates all of that

You've got this.
