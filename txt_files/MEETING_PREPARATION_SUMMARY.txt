   KEY TECHNICAL DECISIONS YOU'LL PRESENT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Decision #1: Dual-BEC Power Architecture (Option B)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Why: ESC BEC alone (3A) is insufficient for worst-case scenarios where multiple servos
stall. Total draw can exceed 2.5A, leaving minimal margin. Brownout risk is unacceptable.

Solution: Add external 5V 6A UBEC dedicated to servos. This isolates high-current servo
loads from flight-critical systems (FC, GPS, Receiver).

Result:
  - Rail 1 (FC + GPS + RX): 663mA typical, 2337mA margin (78%)
  - Rail 2 (Servos): 720mA typical, 4800mA margin (80%)
  - If servo BEC fails, FC stays powered → can glide to landing
  - If FC BEC fails, catastrophic → but protected by isolation

Cost: $12-15 for UBEC
Benefit: Prevents mid-flight brownout → protects $500 aircraft

This is the correct engineering choice.


Decision #2: Common Ground Topology
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Why: All electrical systems must share the same ground reference to prevent ground loops
and ensure clean signal integrity.

Implementation: Star grounding from battery negative. All component grounds connect to
a common ground bus, which connects to battery negative.

Testing: Use multimeter continuity test to verify 0Ω resistance between battery negative
and all component grounds.


Decision #3: Electrical Noise Mitigation
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Why: Motor/ESC switching generates high-frequency noise that can interfere with GPS lock
and analog sensors (MQ135).

Mitigation:
  - Twist motor wires together (reduces electromagnetic field)
  - Route motor wires away from GPS antenna (>10cm separation)
  - Route motor wires away from MQ135 sensor
  - Test GPS lock during motor operation (validation)

If noise persists: Add ferrite beads to motor power lines.

   MEETING FLOW (2.5-3 Hours)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Phase 1: Opening & Architecture Lecture (0:00 - 0:45)
  • Set professional tone with opening statement
  • Draw power flow block diagram on whiteboard as you explain
  • Teach design principles: Isolation, Redundancy, Common Ground, Noise Isolation, Margin

Phase 2: Technical Decision & Organization (0:45 - 1:15)
  • Present three power architecture options (A, B, C)
  • Facilitate discussion, take vote
  • Commit to Option B (Dual-BEC)
  • Assign subsystem teams (Power, Flight Electronics, Research, Integration)

Phase 3: **BREAK** (1:15 - 1:25)
  • 10 minutes - let team absorb information

Phase 4: Safety Training (1:25 - 1:45)
  • LiPo battery safety (charging, storage, fire procedures)
  • Propeller safety (NO PROP INDOORS)
  • Electrical safety (soldering, short circuits, multimeter usage)
  • Sign-off: All team members sign Safety_Protocol.txt

Phase 5: Hands-On Work (1:45 - 2:35)
  • Inventory & Inspection (20 min): Check all components, log in spreadsheet
  • Power Demo (20 min): Build test bench, measure ESC BEC output, test servos

Phase 6: Planning & Closing (2:35 - 3:00)
  • Define Week 1-2 milestones for each subsystem
  • Confirm next meeting date/time
  • Collect signed safety protocols
  • Closing statement: "Go build something great."

   LEADERSHIP PRINCIPLES FOR YOU

BE CONFIDENT
  You are the expert. You've done the analysis. You know the architecture. Your team
  is looking to YOU for technical leadership. Be decisive.

BE CLEAR
  Say exactly what needs to happen, by when, and who's responsible. Avoid vague language
  like "maybe we should..." Use "We will..." or "The requirement is..."

BE SUPPORTIVE
  When someone gets stuck, help them learn. Don't just give the answer. Ask guiding
  questions. They'll remember the lesson better.

BE FIRM ON SAFETY
  Safety is non-negotiable. If someone wants to skip the LiPo bag or test with propeller
  indoors, shut it down immediately. No exceptions.

BE ACCOUNTABLE
  If the project succeeds, credit the team. If it fails, take responsibility. That's
  what leaders do.
